## What PokePay Identity Verification Actually Is

PokePay is a Hong Kong–issued Visa card platform that lets you deposit crypto (USDT, USDC, BTC, ETH) and spend it like regular money anywhere Visa is accepted. Because it sits inside the regulated financial system — the issuer holds Hong Kong MSO, US MSB, Canada MSB, and Poland VASP licenses — it cannot legally hand you a payment card without knowing who you are. That is where KYC, or "Know Your Customer," comes in.

KYC is not a PokePay invention. It is the same anti-money-laundering requirement every licensed financial platform has to follow. PokePay's privacy agreement spells it out plainly: "We carry out checks to verify your identity during onboarding in order to comply with Know Your Customer 'KYC' obligations under anti-money laundering laws." So when you hit the identity verification step, you are not being singled out — you are going through the same gate every user has to pass.

The good news is that PokePay runs a two-tier system, and the tier you need depends entirely on which card you want.

## The Two-Tier System: L1 KYC for Virtual, L2 KYC for Physical

This is the part most beginners get wrong, so let's get it out of the way first.

**L1 KYC — for the virtual card.** This is the lighter check. You provide basic personal information, upload the front and back of an ID document (national ID, driver's license, or passport), and complete a quick face recognition scan. The bar is relatively low, and most users report getting approved within minutes. One thing to keep in mind: some older community guides claim L1 is "casual" and you can "fill in whatever," but that is outdated advice — match your info to your document or you will fail.

**L2 KYC — for the physical card.** This is the stricter check. You need a higher-quality ID upload (passport is the safest bet), a clearer selfie, and the system is less forgiving about lighting, glare, or mismatched names. The reason is simple: the physical card gets mailed to a real address, can be used at ATMs and POS terminals, and carries higher fraud risk for the issuer, so they verify harder.

> Practical takeaway: if you only want a virtual card for online subscriptions, L1 is all you need. If you want the plastic card in your wallet for travel and cash withdrawals, budget extra time for L2.

## Documents You Should Have Ready Before You Start

Nothing kills momentum faster than starting KYC and realizing you do not have the right file on hand. Before you open the verification page, get these ready:

- A government-issued photo ID — passport is the most universally accepted, followed by national ID and driver's license

- A well-lit environment for the selfie / face scan
- The Chrome browser on desktop, or the PokePay app on mobile, with camera permission enabled — PokePay's own FAQ recommends this combo for the smoothest experience

One detail that trips up a lot of international users: if you are using a passport, fill in your name in English exactly as it appears on the passport. Mixed-case or reordered names are a top rejection reason.

## Why People Get Rejected (And How to Fix It)

Rejection is the part nobody likes to talk about, but it happens often enough that you should know the usual suspects before you submit.

**Name mismatch.** The single most common reason. If your ID says "ZHANG SAN" and you typed "San Zhang" or "Zhang San" with different spacing, the automated check fails. Fix: copy the name field character-for-character from your document.

**Blurry or glare-affected ID photos.** The system uses OCR to read the document. Reflections on a laminated ID card will scramble the read. Fix: take the photo in natural light, no flash, on a flat dark background.

**Selfie lighting too dark or face partially out of frame.** Face recognition needs both eyes, your nose, and your mouth visible. Fix: face a window, hold the phone at eye level, and remove glasses or hats.

**Wrong document type for the tier.** Trying to use a student ID or an expired document for L2 will get auto-rejected. Fix: use a valid passport or national ID.

**Restricted jurisdiction.** PokePay does not operate in the US, Russia, China, Cuba, Iran, Iraq, North Korea, Syria, Afghanistan, and a list of other restricted regions. If your ID shows one of these countries, verification will fail regardless of how clean your upload is. Check the restricted list before you start.

If you do get rejected, do not panic. PokePay lets you resubmit. Fix the issue, re-upload, and try again. Most users clear it on the second attempt.
